# Legal Research Workflows

Use this for open-ended legal research or building a legal argument.

## Issue Research

1. Restate the legal issue.
2. Confirm jurisdiction and court level.
3. Identify controlling sources:
   - constitutions
   - statutes
   - rules
   - binding cases
4. Search broadly to map the issue.
5. Search precisely with terms and connectors.
6. Filter to the governing jurisdiction.
7. Read leading authorities.
8. Shepardize key authorities.
9. Expand through headnotes, citing decisions, and tables of authorities.
10. Summarize the answer with confidence level and caveats.

## Authority Bank

For each authority, record:

- citation
- court/source
- rule or holding
- best pincite
- treatment
- proposition supported
- likely use in draft
- risks or limits

## Statute / Rule Research

1. Search the code or rule citation directly.
2. Confirm current text.
3. Read the relevant subsection.
4. Check annotations or citing decisions.
5. Shepardize or otherwise review treatment.
6. Confirm the procedural use in the draft.
7. Flag timing, notice, jurisdiction, standing, or waiver issues.

## Secondary Sources

Use secondary sources to:

- orient on unfamiliar issues
- find leading cases
- confirm procedural frameworks
- identify elements, burdens, standards, and exceptions

Unless the user says secondary sources control their research, do not treat them as substitutes for primary law.

## Draft-Support Research

When researching for a brief section:

1. Identify the sentence or argument needing support.
2. Find the narrowest authority that supports it.
3. Prefer binding primary law.
4. Add secondary authority only where it helps explain the framework.
5. Provide citation and pincite.
6. Note how the authority should be used in the argument.

## Litigation Memo Workflow

Use this for attorney-facing research on motions, notices, objections, demurrers, jurisdiction, timing, or multi-case coordination.

1. Identify the procedural vehicle, deadline, governing standard, and forum first.
2. Separate procedural issues from merits, demurrer, jurisdiction, or substantive claim issues.
3. Organize authorities in this order:
   - controlling rules and statutes
   - controlling appellate cases
   - persuasive trial-court orders or secondary sources
4. For multi-case proceedings, verify where each notice, motion, or petition was filed; whether the response must be filed in every listed pending case; which counsel is authorized to appear in each forum; and whether relation, consolidation, transfer, reassignment, or coordination are distinct remedies.
5. If the issue is not clean, use a neutral risk matrix:
   - governing factor
   - facts favoring the opposing position
   - facts favoring the client
   - objective risk assessment
6. Do not rely on broad jurisdiction labels alone. Tie exclusivity, venue, assignment, or department issues back to the governing procedural standard.

## Output For Research Memo

Use this structure:

1. Short answer
2. Best authorities
3. Analysis
4. Risks and caveats
5. Recommended draft language
6. Authorities reviewed

Keep materials reviewed, search logs, Lexis AI lead-generation notes, Shepard/treatment notes, and rejected authorities in separate working memos unless the user requests them in the main memo.
